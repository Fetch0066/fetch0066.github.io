One origin of the emoji came from a man named Scott Fahlman. He and many others at Carnegie Mellon University would discuss on “online billboards”. The problem came when jokes and humorous remarks became frequently misinterpreted, thus Fahlman proposed this:

PLATO: The First Digital Emoticons?

Some people subscribe to the idea that PLATO was one of the early progenitor’s of the emoji. PLATO was the first widespread computer assisted learning program. It was also one of the earliest internet communities to exist.

The fourth iteration, PLUTO IV, brought hardware-assisted graphics to the program, allowing a student named Bruce Parello to create the first digital emojis on the system.

NTT DOCOMO & Kurita: The First Emoji Set?

Many like to point to the original emoji set coming from artist Shigetaka Kurita. The artist took inspiration from manga, and existing dingbats and glyphs to create an emoji set for NTT DOCOMO devices in 1999. This conception is even popular among museums like the Computer History Museum in Mountain View, who holds a mini emoji exhibition, and Museum of Modern Art in New York, who holds the original emojis from Kurita.

SoftBank & The First Emoji Set

As research has pointed out however, the first emoji set from Japan may have come from a company called SoftBank in 1997. These were created for their own device, the J-Phone. Kurita himself would tweet that his work was not the first, but instead the J-Phone. However the device wasn’t successful compared to DOCOMO, so the proliferation of the emoji most like originated from the latter’s set.

Unicode & Emoji Adoption

The Unicode Standard is a text encoding system handled by the Unicode Consortium. Beforehand, computers would have their own text encoding standards that had limitations and conflicts with other people. Unicode standardized this encoding across all computers.

In 2007, in order to avoid a similar situation with emojis, a symbols subcommitee was created, that then accepted In 2009, a group of Google and Apple employees submitted an official proposal to adopt 625 emoji characters into the standard. It was accepted in 2010.

The Consortium now opens up proposals for new emojis each year.

Other Emoji Events

2011

  • Apple added an official emoji keyboard to IOS.
  • Android would add their official emoji two years later.

2013

  • Emojipedia was released, detailing all the emojis added to the Unicode standard.

2015

  • Emojination was created with the goal of adding more inclusive emojis to the standard.
  • The Emoji standard diversifies with the addition same-sex couple emotes and 5 new skin tones for emojis.

2017

  • The Emoji Movie existed.
  • Paul D Hunt created Unicode’s first gender-inclusive emoji, and has only made more inclusive emojis since.

2019

  • The Emoji Story was released, a documentary about the Emoji’s history.

2023

  • The Computer History Museum launches “Little Emoji, Big Story” a small emoji exhibit supplemented by various events.
